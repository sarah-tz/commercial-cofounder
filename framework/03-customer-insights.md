# Customer Insights: Pains, Gains, and Jobs-to-be-Done

This step takes everything you believe about your customer and organizes it into a structured map. It's based on Strategyzer's Value Proposition Design framework.

At this stage, many of your "insights" are actually assumptions. That's fine — the point is to make them explicit and structured so you can test them systematically.

## The Customer Map

Three categories:

### Pains
What makes getting the job done difficult, frustrating, or expensive?

- What do they find too costly? (time, money, effort)
- What makes them feel bad? (frustrations, annoyances, headaches)
- What current solutions underperform for them?
- What risks do they fear?

**Be specific.** Not "it's hard to manage projects" but "they lose track of client feedback because it comes in across email, Slack, and Notion, and they waste 30 min/day hunting for the latest version."

### Gains
What positive outcomes do they want?

- What would make their job easier?
- What would delight them or exceed expectations?
- What would make them look good to their boss/peers/customers?
- What would save them time, money, or effort?

**Distinguish required gains from nice-to-haves.** A required gain is table stakes — without it, your solution won't even be considered. A nice-to-have is what delights.

### Jobs-to-be-Done
What are they fundamentally trying to accomplish?

- **Functional jobs:** The practical task (process invoices, onboard users, ship code)
- **Emotional jobs:** How they want to feel (in control, confident, less stressed)
- **Social jobs:** How they want to be perceived (competent, innovative, responsible)

**Go deeper than the obvious.** The obvious job for a project management tool user is "manage projects." The deeper job might be "stop feeling anxious about missing deadlines and looking disorganized to clients."

## How to Map

1. **Dump everything.** Write every pain, gain, and job you can think of. Don't filter yet.
2. **Rank by intensity.** Which pains are most severe? Which gains matter most? Which jobs are most important?
3. **Identify the extremes.** The most extreme pains and most desired gains are where the opportunity lives.
4. **Mark what's validated vs. assumed.** Be honest about what you actually know from real customer conversations vs. what you're guessing.

## Where These Insights Come From

- **Customer interviews** (best source — but most founders haven't done enough of these)
- **Your own experience** (valid if you're solving your own problem, but watch for bias)
- **Public forums** (Reddit, Twitter, Indie Hackers, Stack Overflow — real people complaining about real problems)
- **Competitor reviews** (what do customers love/hate about existing solutions?)
- **Support tickets/feedback** (if you have an existing product)

## What to Do with This

Your customer map is the raw material for assumption extraction. Every "I believe" statement about your customer's pains, gains, and JTBD is an assumption that needs to be tested.
