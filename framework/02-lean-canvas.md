# LEAN Canvas

The LEAN Canvas (Ash Maurya) captures your entire business model on one page. It forces you to articulate your hypothesis about every critical piece of your business — and makes gaps obvious.

## When to Use Which

**LEAN(er) Canvas** — Use this if you're at the Idea stage or very early Problem/Solution Fit. It focuses only on the customer side: Problem, Current Alternatives, Customer Segment, and Early Adopters.

**Full LEAN Canvas** — Use this once you have enough clarity on your customer to start thinking about your solution, channels, revenue model, and cost structure.

If in doubt, start with the LEAN(er) Canvas. You can always expand later.

## LEAN(er) Canvas

Four sections, all focused on the customer:

### Problem
What's broken with the status quo? What are the top 1-3 problems your customer faces? Be specific — "inefficiency" is not a problem. "Spending 3 hours/week manually reconciling invoices because their accounting tool doesn't integrate with their payment processor" is a problem.

### Current Alternatives
How is the job getting done today? These are your real competitors — not just other startups, but spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring someone, or doing nothing. Understanding current alternatives tells you what you're actually replacing.

### Customer Segment
Who is this for? What job do they want done? This is your broader target market that you'll eventually grow into.

### Early Adopters
Your beachhead — the subset of your customer segment who need this the most, right now. These are the people for whom your solution is a no-brainer. This should align tightly with your W1 answer.

## Full LEAN Canvas

Nine boxes:

| Left Side (Product) | | Right Side (Market) |
|---------------------|--|---------------------|
| **Problem** | **Value Proposition** | **Unfair Advantage** |
| Top 1-3 problems | Single, compelling message | Can't be easily copied/bought |
| **Current Alternatives** | | **Customer Segments** |
| How it's done today | | Target customers |
| **Solution** | | **Early Adopters** |
| Top 3 features | | Your beachhead |
| **Key Metrics** | | **Channels** |
| What you measure | | How you reach them |
| **Cost Structure** | | **Revenue Streams** |
| What it costs to run | | How you make money |

### Tips for Each Box

- **Value Proposition:** Use your articulated value prop from the `/valueprop` exercise. If you can't say it in one sentence, keep working on it.
- **Unfair Advantage:** You probably don't have one yet, and that's fine. Leave it blank or write "TBD." Real unfair advantages emerge over time — from deep customer knowledge, proprietary data, network effects, or brand. Don't force it.
- **Key Metrics:** At this stage, pick your MIK (Most Important KPI). Don't list 15 metrics.
- **Solution:** Resist the urge to describe your full product. List only the minimal features needed to test your hypothesis.
- **Channels:** How will you actually reach your early adopters? "Social media" is not a channel. "DMs to founders in Indie Hackers who posted about X problem in the last month" is a channel.
- **Revenue Streams:** Be specific. "$X/month subscription" or "Y% commission on transactions." If you don't know yet, write your best hypothesis.
- **Cost Structure:** What does it actually cost to run this? Include your time.

## Multiple Segments?

If you're still exploring different early adopter segments, fill out a separate LEAN(er) Canvas for each. This helps you compare and choose. But you must choose. You cannot serve multiple segments at this stage — you don't have the resources, bandwidth, or clarity to do it well.

## What to Do with This

Your canvas is a hypothesis, not a plan. Every box contains assumptions that need testing. The next step is to extract those assumptions explicitly.
