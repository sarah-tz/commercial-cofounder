# Assumptions: Identify, Categorize, Prioritize

This is where the real work begins. Everything you've done so far — W3, canvas, customer map — is a collection of hypotheses. Now you make every belief explicit and figure out which ones could kill your business if they're wrong.

## What Is an Assumption?

An assumption is something you believe to be true but haven't yet backed up with evidence. Every startup is built on a stack of assumptions. The dangerous ones are the ones you don't realize you're making.

## Step 1: Extract Your Assumptions

Go through each of your previous outputs and pull out every belief statement:

**From your W3 answers:**
- "I believe [these people] have [this problem]"
- "I believe they currently solve it by [doing X]"
- "I believe they would pay for [Y] because [Z]"

**From your LEAN Canvas:**
- "I believe the top problem is..."
- "I believe I can reach customers through..."
- "I believe they'll pay $X/month..."
- "I believe it will cost me $Y to acquire a customer"

**From your Customer Map:**
- "I believe the biggest pain is..."
- "I believe the most important job is..."
- "I believe they care about [this gain]"

Be exhaustive. You'll typically end up with 20-50 assumptions. That's normal.

## Step 2: Categorize

Group your assumptions into three buckets:

### Desirability — Do customers want this?
- Does the problem exist and matter enough?
- Is our solution something they'd actually use/buy?
- Are there enough customers who need this?

### Feasibility — Can we actually deliver?
- Can we build this with our current skills/resources?
- Can we deliver at the quality level needed?
- Can we do it in a reasonable timeframe?

### Viability — Does the business work?
- Can we make money doing this?
- Are the unit economics workable?
- Can we acquire customers at a sustainable cost?
- Is the market big enough?

**For most early-stage startups, start with Desirability.** If nobody wants what you're building, Feasibility and Viability don't matter.

## Step 3: Prioritize

Plot each assumption on two axes:

**Y-axis: Importance** — If this assumption is wrong, how badly does it hurt the business?
- Top = business-critical (if wrong, we're dead)
- Bottom = nice to know (if wrong, we adjust)

**X-axis: Evidence** — How much proof do we have?
- Left = strong evidence (validated through data/interviews)
- Right = no evidence (pure guess)

```
        IMPORTANT
            │
   Validated│ ← TEST THESE FIRST
   but need │   (Important + No evidence)
   more     │
   evidence │
            │
            ├────────────────────────
            │
   Nice     │  Don't worry about
   to know  │  these yet
            │
        NOT IMPORTANT
     EVIDENCE ←───────────→ NO EVIDENCE
```

**The top-right quadrant is your danger zone.** These are assumptions that are critical to your business AND have no supporting evidence. Test these first.

## Common Dangerous Assumptions

Most founders underestimate these:

1. **"People will pay for this"** — The most common untested assumption. Interest ≠ willingness to pay.
2. **"I can reach my customers through [channel]"** — Having a great product means nothing if you can't get it in front of people.
3. **"The problem is big enough to justify switching"** — People tolerate a lot of pain before they'll change their behavior.
4. **"My early adopters represent the broader market"** — Your friends who said "I'd totally buy that" are not a market.
5. **"I can build this as a solo founder"** — Time, skills, and energy are finite.

## What to Do with This

Take your top 3-5 riskiest assumptions (important + unproven) and turn them into experiments. Each assumption becomes a hypothesis to test.
