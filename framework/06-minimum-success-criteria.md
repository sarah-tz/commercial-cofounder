# Minimum Success Criteria

Instead of dreaming about how big your startup *could* get, think about the smallest outcome that would make you consider it a success in three years or less.

## The Levels (Fermi Estimates)

| Level | ARR | What It Means |
|-------|-----|---------------|
| 1 | $100K | Quit your day job |
| 2 | $1M | Small 3-5 person company |
| 3 | $10M | VC-investible business |
| 4 | $100M | Potential unicorn |

Pick the level that represents *your* minimum success. Be honest — not every business needs to be VC-scale, and not every founder wants that.

## Traction Sizing

Now work backwards from your target. If you need $1M ARR:

- At $50/month per customer = 1,667 customers
- At $200/month per customer = 417 customers
- At $2,000/month per customer = 42 customers

This math is revealing. It tells you:
- **Is your pricing model realistic?** Can you actually get that many customers?
- **Does your market support this?** Are there enough potential customers?
- **Is your acquisition model feasible?** Can you reach and convert that many people?

If the numbers don't work, your business model needs to change. Better to discover that now than after 18 months of building.

## How to Calculate

1. **Pick your MSC level** (the minimum ARR that makes this worth doing)
2. **Define your pricing model** (what you charge, how often)
3. **Calculate required customers** (MSC / annual revenue per customer)
4. **Estimate your addressable market** (how many potential customers exist)
5. **Calculate required market share** (required customers / addressable market)
6. **Gut check:** Does this market share feel achievable? If you need >5% of the market, your niche might be too small or your pricing too low.

## Common Revelations

After doing this exercise, founders often realize:
- Their price point is too low to build a real business
- They need way more customers than they can realistically acquire
- Their market is actually too small for their ambitions
- They should be going B2B instead of B2C (or vice versa)

These are all good realizations. They save you months of building the wrong thing.

## What to Do with This

If the numbers work: great, you have a target to aim for. If they don't: revisit your pricing, market, or business model before investing more time building.

Either way, add any new assumptions this exercise revealed to your assumptions map.
