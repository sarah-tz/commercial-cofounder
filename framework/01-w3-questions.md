# W3 Questions: Who, What, Why

The W3 Questions (from TechStars / "Levers" by Amos Schwartzfarb) are the foundation of everything that follows. Get these wrong and everything downstream is built on sand.

## The Questions

1. **WHO** is your customer?
2. **WHAT** are they buying from you?
3. **WHY** are they buying it?

## Why Your First Answers Are Wrong

Most founders answer these in about 30 seconds and end up with something like:
- **Who:** Developers
- **What:** Our API product
- **Why:** Because it saves them time

These answers are useless. They're too broad to act on, they describe what *you* sell (not what the customer *buys*), and the "why" is generic enough to apply to literally any product.

## How to Go Deeper

### W1: WHO — Your Beachhead Customer

Don't describe a market. Describe a person so specific that you know almost every conversation with them will result in a sale.

**Bad:** "Small business owners"
**Better:** "Solo consultants running a 1-person agency making $100-300K/year who currently manage their client pipeline in spreadsheets and are losing deals because they can't follow up fast enough"

Ask yourself:
- What's their role/title?
- What's the size/type of their organization?
- What behavior or situation makes them a buyer?
- What's their current way of solving this problem?
- Why is NOW the moment they need a solution?

### W2: WHAT — What They're Actually Buying

This is NOT what you're selling. It's the job they're hiring your product to do.

Customers don't buy products. They hire solutions to get a job done. A drill buyer doesn't want a drill — they want a hole. But even that's only part of it — they might want the hole to hang a picture, and what they really want is a cozy living space.

**Types of jobs:**
- **Functional:** The practical task (get from A to B)
- **Emotional:** How they want to feel (feel in control, feel safe)
- **Social:** How they want to be perceived (look competent, fit in)

Ask yourself:
- What job is the customer trying to get done?
- What's the deeper job behind the obvious one?
- What alternatives are they currently "hiring" for this job?
- Why aren't those alternatives good enough?

### W3: WHY — The Deeper Motivation

Why goes beyond the job. It's about how the customer measures success and justifies the purchase.

A brand manager might buy your marketing tool not because it's clever, but because it directly improves the KPI their boss evaluates them on. Understanding that changes everything about how you sell.

Ask yourself:
- What value do they get from using your product?
- How do they know they're getting that value?
- How do they measure it?
- What happens if they don't solve this problem?

## Examples

**Mental Health App (weak):**
- Who: People with anxiety
- What: Access to our app
- Why: To help with their depression

**Mental Health App (strong):**
- Who: Young people in a pivotal crisis moment who recognize their need for mental health support but can't access it due to waitlists/costs. They're introverted and not comfortable sharing with people (including therapists) until trust is built.
- What: An always-present companion when they have no one to talk to during critical moments and really need it.
- Why: They desperately need moments of serenity and clarity, and traditional channels are either inaccessible or require a level of vulnerability they're not ready for.

Notice how the strong version gives you everything you need to build, market, and sell — while the weak version gives you nothing.

## What to Do with This

Your W3 answers will evolve as you learn. That's expected. But having a specific, thought-out starting hypothesis is what makes everything else possible. These answers feed directly into your LEAN Canvas, your assumption map, and your experiment design.
