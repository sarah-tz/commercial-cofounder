# Value Proposition

You need to be able to explain what your startup does in a way that makes people instantly get it. If your listener's eyes glaze over, you've failed — not because your idea is bad, but because you haven't distilled it yet.

## The Mad Lib Template

From Ash Maurya's LEANSTACK:

> When **[customers]** encounter a **[triggering event]**, they need to **[job-to-be-done]** to achieve **[desired outcome]**.
>
> They would normally use **[existing alternatives]**, but because of **[switching trigger]**, these alternatives no longer work because of **[these problems]**. If these problems are left unaddressed, then **[what's at stake]**.
>
> So we built a solution that helps **[customers]** achieve **[desired outcome]** by helping them **[unique value proposition]**.

This template forces you to connect every piece: customer, trigger, job, alternatives, problem, stakes, and your unique value.

## Value Proposition vs. Unique Value Proposition

A **value proposition** explains what value you deliver.

A **unique value proposition** goes further — it says you deliver this value in a way customers perceive as superior to alternatives, ideally in a way nobody else can.

You might not have a unique value proposition yet. That's expected at this stage. You're still learning what makes your approach genuinely different. The uniqueness often emerges from deep customer understanding, not from brainstorming sessions.

## On Unfair Advantages

You probably don't have one yet. That's fine.

Early-stage startups almost never have a real unfair advantage. What you think is your moat (first-mover, technology, team experience) is usually easily replicated.

Real unfair advantages come from things like:
- Deep, proprietary customer knowledge
- Network effects that grow with usage
- Exclusive data or relationships
- Brand that took years to build

The only unfair advantage that matters right now: **learning faster than everyone else and acting on what you learn.**

Don't force an unfair advantage into your pitch. Focus on being the best solution for a very specific customer with a very specific problem.

## Testing Your Value Prop

You know your value proposition is working when you transition from "hoping they'll buy" to "knowing they'll buy."

Signs it's working:
- People immediately understand what you do
- They say "oh, I need that" without you having to convince them
- They can explain it to someone else accurately
- They ask "how do I get started?" not "what exactly does it do?"

Signs it needs work:
- You need more than 30 seconds to explain it
- People say "interesting" and change the subject
- You get lots of "let me know when it's ready" but no commitments
- Different people think you do completely different things

## What to Do with This

Write your best current version. It will evolve — that's the point. But having a concrete starting hypothesis lets you test it in real conversations and iterate.
